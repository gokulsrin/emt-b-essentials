## Front

What are the classic findings of <b>acute decompensated CHF</b> / <b>pulmonary edema</b> in the field, and how do you position and treat the patient?

## Back

<b>Classic findings:</b>
- Severe SOB, worse lying flat (<b>orthopnea</b>) or waking at night (PND)
- <b>Crackles / rales</b> at lung bases; sometimes <u>"pink frothy sputum"</u>
- <u>JVD</u> (jugular venous distension)
- Pedal / sacral edema, rapid weight gain
- Hypertensive, tachycardic, anxious; low SpO₂

<b>Field treatment:</b>
- Sit the patient upright with legs dependent (never lay flat)
- O₂ titrated to SpO₂ ≥ 94% (NRB or BVM if failing)
- <b>CPAP</b> if available and within scope
- Assist with prescribed nitroglycerin if SBP > 100 and within protocol
- Rapid transport; reassess every 5 minutes

## Extra

CHF/pulmonary edema can mimic asthma/COPD at first glance.
Clues that say cardiac: cardiac history, wet lung sounds (crackles, not wheezes alone),
JVD, leg edema, and worsening when supine.
